\subsubsection{Equivalent circuit model}\label{sec:monopole_eqc}
\FloatBarrier

Equivalent circuit models are valuable tools for further analysis, as they allow 
the coupling mechanisms between the antenna and the TEM cell to be expressed in 
terms of measurable lumped circuit parameters. For the monopole antenna, the 
dominant coupling mechanism is capacitive: the open-ended wire accumulates charge, 
drives a displacement current toward the septum, and couples to the TEM mode. 
The antenna's impedance behavior is therefore modeled by an RLC series circuit 
for a short electric dipole \cite[p.~8]{Chu_1948}, as shown in 
\autoref{fig:eqc_simple_monopole}. In this circuit, the capacitance $C_A$ 
dominates the antenna impedance at low frequencies, the inductance $L_A$ 
represents the stored magnetic energy near the wire, and the resistance $R_A$ 
accounts for radiated power. At low frequencies, $L_A$ is short-circuited by 
the low reactance of $C_A$, so that the voltage drops predominantly across $C_A$ 
\cite[p.~8]{Chu_1948}. As frequency increases, the influence of the radiation 
resistance $R_A$ grows and the overall impedance falls, consistent with the 
capacitively dominated behavior discussed in \cref{sec:monopole_impedance}.

Of primary concern for further analysis are $L_A$ and $C_A$, which are determined with the antenna model placed on a PEC surface in free space, as demonstrated in \autoref{fig:free-space-monopole}. The feed current $I_{in}$ is computed according to \eqref{eqn:iin}. The inductance and capacitance are derived according to 
\eqref{eqn:l_m_energy} and \eqref{eqn:c_e_energy}, which leads to 

\begin{subequations}
	\begin{equation}
		L_A = 2\frac{W_m}{I_{LA}^2},
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		C_A = 2\frac{W_c}{V_{CA}^2},
	\end{equation}
\end{subequations}

where $V_{CA}$ denotes the voltage across the capacitor 
$C_A$, and $I_{LA}$ denotes the current through the inductor $L_A$. The resulting capacitance equals $C_A=98.36\,\mathrm{fF}$, and the inductance amounts to $L_A=1.14\,\mathrm{nH}$. The resistance $R_A$ generally represents conduction and radiation losses. However, only radiation losses are relevant here, as conduction losses are non-existent due to the use of PECs. The resistance $R_A$ can thereby be derived solely from the effective current through the resistance $I_\mathrm{RA,eff}$ and the radiated power $P_\mathrm{rad}$ along the radiation boundary, as expressed in 

\begin{equation}
	R_A = \frac{P_\mathrm{rad}}{I_\mathrm{RA,eff}^2}.
\end{equation}

The resulting radiation resistance spans from the milliohm range up to 
$0.9\,\Omega$ in the investigated frequency range and increases 
quadratically with frequency.

The antenna equivalent circuit is extended in \autoref{fig:full_circuit_monopole} 
to incorporate the TEM cell's electrical characteristics, represented by a total inductance $L_T = L_{T1} + L_{T2}$ and capacitance 
$C_T = C_{T1} + C_{T2}$, as derived in \cref{sec:tem_cell_model}. The components are split symmetrically ($L_{T1} = L_{T2}$ and $C_{T1} = C_{T2}$) to reflect the physical symmetry of the TEM cell. The source provides a constant input power $P_{in}=1\,\mathrm{W}$ and has a source resistance of $R_s=50\,\Omega$, representing the characteristic impedance of the feedpoint waveport in the simulation model.

The capacitance $C_k$ models the 
electric coupling path leading from the antenna to the TEM cell. Displacement current from the monopole antenna injects 
charge into the TEM cell, driving the electric dipole moment $\mathbf{m}_e$ as 
described in \cref{sec:el_small_antennas}. The mutual inductances $M_{A,T1}$ 
and $M_{A,T2}$ model the magnetic coupling path. The magnetic flux shared between the antenna 
inductance $L_A$ and the TEM cell inductances $L_{T1}$ and $L_{T2}$ induces 
voltages across the septum, driving the magnetic dipole moment $\mathbf{m}_m$. 
Together, these two elements fully represent the near-field power transfer to 
the output ports,making the inclusion of a separate radiation resistance $R_A$ in the combined circuit unnecessary.

Since the antenna and TEM cell inductors share magnetic flux, their branch 
voltages are coupled. The relationship between the voltages and currents  
for the three inductive branches is expressed as

\begin{equation}
	\mathbf{V} = j\omega \begin{bmatrix}
		L_{A}     & M_{A,T1} & M_{A,T2} \\
		M_{T1,A}  & L_{T1}   & 0 \\
		M_{T2,A}  & 0        & L_{T2}
	\end{bmatrix} \mathbf{I},
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{I}=\left[ I_{LA}, I_{LT1}, I_{LT2} \right]^T$ denotes the 
current vector and $\mathbf{V}=\left[ V_{LA}, V_{LT1}, V_{LT2} \right]^T$ 
the voltage vector. The coupling is assumed to be reciprocal, as no active 
devices or nonreciprocal media are present \cite[p.~175]{Pozar_2012}. The 
mutual inductances are indicated in \autoref{fig:full_circuit_monopole} 
by the dots next to the inductor symbols.

Analogously, the electric dipole moment $\mathbf{m}_e$ is derived from the displacement current flowing through $C_k$. The coupling components $C_k$, $M_{A,T1}$, and $M_{A,T2}$ are determined by defining the input and output voltages according to the results obtained from numerical simulations and solving the circuit equations for these unknowns. For the monopole antenna under investigation, the mutual inductances are found to be negligible, $M_{A,T1} = M_{A,T2} = 0$, while the coupling capacitance is approximately $C_k \approx 1.68,\mathrm{pF}$.

The equivalent dipole moments are extracted from the circuit using a two-step procedure for each coupling mechanism. The following description covers the general case, including magnetic coupling, even though the monopole antenna under investigation does not exhibit a magnetic dipole moment. Consequently, the procedure can be applied to arbitrary antennas.

First, the relevant source quantity is determined. For the magnetic dipole moment, this quantity is the induced voltage across the septum inductances $L_{T1}$ and $L_{T2}$. For the electric dipole moment, it is the displacement current injected through the coupling capacitance $C_k$. In the second step, Kirchhoff's laws are applied to determine the fraction of this quantity that reaches the termination resistances $R_1$ and $R_2$ at the output ports.

The magnetic dipole moment $\mathbf{m}_m$ is given by \eqref{eqn:m_v}, which relates it to the voltage appearing across the termination resistances due to magnetic coupling. In the equivalent circuit, this coupling originates from the mutual inductances $M_{A,T1}$ and $M_{A,T2}$, which induce voltages across the TEM cell inductances $L_{T1}$ and $L_{T2}$. However, not all of this induced voltage appears across $R_1$ and $R_2$, since part of it drops across $L_{T1}$ and $L_{T2}$ themselves. Applying Kirchhoff's voltage law to the branch formed by $L_{T1}$, $C_{T1}$, and $R_1$ (and analogously to the $L_{T2}$, $C_{T2}$, $R_2$ branch) yields the voltage at the termination resistances required by \eqref{eqn:m_v}.

The electric dipole moment $\mathbf{m}_e$ is expressed by \eqref{eqn:me_i}, which relates it to the current flowing through the termination resistances due to electric coupling. This current is obtained analogously by applying Kirchhoff's current law to determine the fraction of the displacement current injected into the TEM cell through the coupling capacitance $C_k$ that flows through $R_1$ and $R_2$.

Consequently, the output voltages $V_{1,2}$ are determined separately for magnetic and electric coupling, resulting in the contributions $V'_{1,2}$ and $V''_{1,2}$, respectively:

\begin{subequations}
	\begin{equation}
		V'_{1,2}=\pm j\omega MI_{LA} \frac{R_{1,2}\parallel(1/j\omega C_{T1,2})}{R_{1,2}\parallel(1/j\omega C_{T1,2})+j\omega L_{T1,2}},
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		V''_{1,2} = \frac{I_k}{2}\frac{1/R_{1,2}}{1/R_{1,2}+j\omega C_{T1,2}},
	\end{equation}
\end{subequations}


where the total output voltage follows from superposition $V_{1,2}=V'_{1,2}+V''_{1,2}$ and $R_{1,2}\parallel(1/j\omega C_{T1,2})$ denotes the parallel impedance of $R_{1,2}$ and $(1/j\omega C_{T1,2})$. The $\pm$ sign distinguishes between the
voltage amplitudes propagating to waveport~1 ($V_1$) and waveport~2 ($V_2$).

For both cases, the power dissipated at the termination resistances is computed separately. Its square root is then taken to obtain the wave amplitude normalized to $\sqrt{\mathrm{W}}$, as required by \eqref{eqn:m_v} and \eqref{eqn:me_i}. This yields the modal coefficients $a_\mathrm{TEM}$ and $b_\mathrm{TEM}$, given by

\begin{subequations}
	\begin{equation}
		a_\mathrm{TEM} = \sqrt{V_1^2/R_1},
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		b_\mathrm{TEM} = \sqrt{V_2^2/R_2}.
	\end{equation}
\end{subequations}

Finally, the equivalent dipole moments are obtained from \eqref{eqn:m_v} and \eqref{eqn:me_i}. The resulting dipole moments for the monopole antenna are shown in \autoref{fig:eqc-dipoles-comp}.

surface in an open space, as shown in \autoref{fig:free-space-loop}. 
The inductance and capacitance are derived according to 
\eqref{eqn:l_m_energy} and \eqref{eqn:c_e_energy}, which leads to

\begin{subequations}

and $L_A=2.14\,\mathrm{nH}$.

the same two-step procedure described in \cref{sec:monopole_eqc}: first,

both the electric and the magnetic coupling mechanisms are active here, as the loop antenna exhibits a significant magnetic dipole moment in addition to the electric one.
